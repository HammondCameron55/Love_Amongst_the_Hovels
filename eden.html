Eden Lyman

Co-Author · Literary Co-Conspirator · Enthusiast of the Early Twentieth Century

Eden Lyman is a writer whose fascination with early twentieth-century Russia is matched only by their capacity to render it simultaneously educational, entertaining, and gently absurd. As a co-author, Eden is responsible for a significant portion of the psychological tension and approximately all of the historically accurate footnotes.

The decision to set Love Amongst the Hovels in early 1900s Russia was, Eden has explained, "obvious" — a period offering maximum scenic bleakness, abundant material for social commentary, and a pre-existing literary tradition substantial enough to either honor or gently poke with a stick, depending on the chapter.

Eden approaches the craft of writing with a seriousness of purpose that stops just short of solemnity, which is precisely where the humor lives. The result is a book that takes its own comedy very seriously indeed, which is either a sophisticated artistic choice or an extremely funny accident.

On the Literary Tradition

Eden has described Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment as "an excellent book that contains a crime and a punishment and an enormous amount of other material that could perhaps have been edited." This observation forms the philosophical cornerstone of Love Amongst the Hovels: what if one kept the psychological richness and the punishment, but replaced the crime with love, and the St. Petersburg setting with a considerably more modest collection of rural hovels?

Known Facts

  • Co-authored an entirely historically accurate work of fiction
  • Holds strong views on the narrative deployment of existential dread
  • Believes early 1900s Russia was a period of enormous literary potential and would like everyone to know this
  • Responsible for the preachiness (takes this responsibility seriously)
  • Has read more of Dostoevsky than strictly required
