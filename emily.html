Emily Lyman

Co-Author · Connoisseur of Culinary Abundance · Early Retiree of the Evening Hours

Emily Lyman is a writer of substantial warmth and very sensible scheduling. Her approach to creative work reflects the same philosophy as her approach to dining: arrive early, select generously from all available options, and depart at a reasonable hour while others are still milling about looking at the dessert table.

As co-author of Love Amongst the Hovels, Emily brought to the project a distinctive narrative sensibility — one shaped by the twin virtues of abundance and timely rest. Literary scholars have noted that the book's most expansive chapters were likely written in the afternoon, and its more abrupt endings in the early evening, just before eight.

Her commitment to the buffet as a dining philosophy is, she insists, a metaphor for her approach to writing: one should never commit to a single narrative thread when several small plates of subplot are available. This argument is more convincing than it has any right to be.

On the Subject of Early Russia

Emily's interest in early twentieth-century Russia can be traced to a single formative observation: that the era contained an extraordinary number of people who were very cold, very serious, and in immediate need of a good meal. She found this both historically significant and personally relatable.

Known Facts

  • Lover of buffet meals — format, philosophy, and execution alike
  • Goes to sleep early; is unapologetic about this
  • Co-authored an entirely historically accurate work of fiction
  • Believes that "reasonable literary significance" is a perfectly respectable target for a novel
  • Has strong opinions about narrative pacing (see also: bedtime)
