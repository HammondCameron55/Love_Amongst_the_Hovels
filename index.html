About the Book

A tome of unquestionable historical rigor

It is the dawn of the twentieth century. Russia is a land of frozen steppes, vigorous mustaches, and an absolutely baffling number of identical peasant hovels. Into this backdrop of simmering historical significance steps a cast of characters whose every action is both deeply symbolic and vaguely uncomfortable.


Drawing generously from the well of Dostoevsky — and by "generously" we mean with the reckless abandon of someone who skimmed the Wikipedia summary — Love Amongst the Hovels delivers all the existential dread you love, none of the lengthy axe-based plot detours you didn't ask for.


Punishment, however, is provided in abundance. You're welcome.

· · ·

📜 A Note on the Humor Within

The wit deployed in Love Amongst the Hovels is best described as dry satirical parody — a blend of literary pastiche, self-deprecating absurdism, and deadpan historical comedy. Think Monty Python writing footnotes for a Dostoevsky novel, or a very earnest museum placard that slowly realizes it has lost control of the narrative.


Key comedic modes include: mock-scholarly solemnity, the confident mislabeling of historical facts, characters who are acutely aware they are in a 19th-century Russian novel and quietly resentful about it, and an authorial voice that treats every trivial domestic scene with the gravity of the Crimean War.
